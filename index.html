F.A.Q.

How does it work?

Fever Alert has access to your heart beat per minute (bpm) using your Apple Watch.
The app automatically checks your heart beat per minute and compares it to the alert threshold you have set

Why using heart rate at rest to monitor body temperature?

Fever Alert is an evidenced-based application. According to scientific literature both in adults (Tanner, J Physiol 1951 ; Victoria, Ann Occup Hyg 2015) and children (Davies, Emerg Med J 2009), there is a direct correlation between heart rate and body temperature.
Fever Alert was designed to help you monitor your heart beat in order to quickly detect fever coming.

Is it reliable?

Fever Alert is an evidence-based application, which was designed based on a review of scientific literature (Tanner, J Physiol 1951 ; Victoria, Ann Occup Hyg 2015 ; Davies, Emerg Med J 2009).
Fever Alert was not designed to replace a thermometer nor it intends to do so.
Fever Alert is not a medical device and does not make any medical claim.

How should I set my fever alert threshold?

Using your Apple Watch and historical records of your average heart rate at rest, Fever Alert recommends you a threshold value. You can either choose the recommended value as pre-set threshold or set it on your own.

What should I do if my heart beat at rest goes above the pre-set threshold?

Fever Alert automatically checks your heart beat per minute and compares it to the alert threshold you have set.
With the premium feature, if your heart rate exceeds the set threshold, you will receive a notification directly on your iPhone and your Apple Watch. It is then recommended to take your body temperature with a thermometer.

In a nutshell, what is the scientific rationale?

According to Victoria et al. [1], there is a correlation between heart rate at rest and body temperature:

As shown in the graph plot above, there is a linear regression between rectal temperature and body temperature (incl. fever), both at work and at rest.
Fever Alert only uses heart rate at rest measures, thus allowing a very low rate of false positive alerts.

[1] Victoria L. Richmond, Sarah Davey, Katy Griggs, George Havenith, Prediction of Core Body Temperature from Multiple Variables, The Annals of Occupational Hygiene, Volume 59, Issue 9, November 2015, Pages 1168–1178
